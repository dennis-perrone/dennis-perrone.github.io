# Post Overview
Organization is one of the new hot topics. It seems that the pandemic only intensified peoples need and want to have their life in order and some resemblance of control. I wanted to write a post about something a little more organic to me than my other posts on technical topics. I love learning and I love technology. In the past, I have had issues when it came to my personal organization. The reason that I mentioned my love of learning and technology is because I feel that these go hand in hand with being organized ... or at least doing them as effectively and efficiently as possible. I have a handful of IT certifications, all that required a good deal of studying, memorization, and application of the knowledge for the relative certification. 

Before I really put in the effort to be organized and deliberate in my study habits, I had a very haphazard way to attack the mountain of information that is required to pass a certification exam. What I found out through this process was that it added an unnecessary level of stress and frustration on top of an already difficult time. This is a long introduction to just say that I needed to get myself organized, but here I am.

# My Path
## The Catalyst
I remember around the 2014 timeframe I was struggling. I was in a very demanding position that required a lot from me both mentally and physically. I was put into a leadership role very early in my career and didn’t have the foundation nor the support to succeed as everybody was just as overworked. The job required a large amount of paperwork that had to be in sync with multiple departments and maintained for audit. I had a co-worker who had a moleskin notebook and he showed me how he used it to offload ideas out of his head onto paper so he wouldn’t forget little bits of information. Until this time, it never occurred to me that this was even an option, let alone one that would send me on a path of constant improvement and working towards being more efficient and organized.

## My challenges and criteria
My work environment was very unlike most common work environments. I worked in an industrial setting where I didn’t always have ready access to a computer or cell phone and when I did, the internet service was very sporadic. I don’t know what online tools were available around that time as it wasn’t a viable option for me, but I remember looking for ways to improve a simple, pocket notebook and make it work for me. The idea that an analog companion will always be there and never require internet, a power source, or anything outside of a pen and paper.

## The Research
In 2013, Ryder Carroll released the concepts of the [Bullet Journal](https://bulletjournal.com/) to the internet which means when I started looking around for solutions, it was still in its infancy. In hindsight, I feel like this was a benefit for me. This was before there were Instagram pages and entire blogs filled with art filled journals. While these are nice to look at, I needed something barebones that worked for me with no frills. Something that got thoughts out of my head and allowed me to organize information and tasks without dropping the ball. There is an awesome video that serves as a great overview of the Bullet Journal system [here](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fm15cmYU0IM&ab_channel=BulletJournal).

## Moving On
I was hooked. Once I started reading about the Bullet Journal (bujo for short), I was all in. It all made sense and was as simple as you made it. Ryder only lists 3 *collections* in the initial conception of a bujo. Those 3 are a *future log*, *monthly log*, and *rapid log (or dailies)*.

## Other Options
It wasn’t until later on in my journey that I started looking at different options and systems. There is another system with a cult like following, and that is [Getting Things Done](https://gettingthingsdone.com/) or GTD for short. There are literally entire books written about GTD, all of which can explain it and its merits much better than I can, but what I love about it is that it is completely agnostic of the medium and instead it relies on a set of processes that make it work for so many people. 

Other interesting trends that seem to have been popping up are how folks are integrating their personal planning and productivity with what would traditionally be project management concepts such a Kanban. At the end of the day, what ever makes the most sense for the person that makes them productive is the right system.

# Continuing Forward
As of the time of this post, I’m currently using a bullet journal to organize all of my life tasks. I’ve seen debates and conversations on both sides, but I currently employ 2 bullet journals. I use one for work and have another one for my home and personal life. In fact, this is such a common question that Ryder Carroll answered it himself in this [YouTube video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xO7S3sCd8rw&ab_channel=BulletJournal). I also implement a Kanban board for longer products that require multiple stages. I found that this hybrid system enables the ability to deep dive into a day-to-day but also the ability to see my 50,000-foot view. I currently use the 3 original collections but also employ a weekly overview so I can see what needs to be done for the week. I don’t have any qualms with writing something more than once. That minor inconvenience is much less of a deal to me than it would be for me to miss an event or a deadline.
