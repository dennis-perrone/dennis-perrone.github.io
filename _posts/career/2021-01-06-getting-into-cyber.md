# The Cyber Security Field … A Primer
There has been a lot of buzz over the last few years about the cyber security field and the lack of qualified professionals to fill all of the open roles. With breaches being a common occurrence, I feel that many people have gotten desensitized their occurrence or possibly see it as an easy way to make a living. Maybe you are already an IT professional and are looking to cross over into a different niche for a change of pace, maybe you are not currently in IT and are looking for a foothold into a critically required field. In this post, I hope to lay out some ways forward in addition to give you some points to think about. In a nut shell though, cyber security is about protecting people and protecting the business.

# Determine Your Niche
There are *tons* of niches within IT, and even just within cyber security. Below are just a few of them and most require completely different skill sets in order to be effective.

* Cloud Security
* Network Security
* Blue Team
* Red Team
* Security Operation Center Work
* Vulnerability Management
* Compliance
    * See my introduction into the [NIST Risk Management Framework](/tech/2020/12/rmf-overview)
* Auditing
* Sales

# Some opening thoughts
Take the possibility of a solid salary and (perceived) job stability out of cyber security and look at what the job entails and what it requires outside of just learning hard skills. This reflection is much more beneficial to you if you’re critical and realistic when answering these questions. 

Questions that somebody should ask themselves prior to making the actual jump are things such as:

* Do I have time to study outside of work to progress within cyber security
* Do I have the initiative to put in that work
* Am I passionate about learning new technologies
* Do I work better or as part of a team (spoiler: There are jobs for both types!)
* Am I comfortable pushing back (respectfully) against authority if I’m correct
* Outside of the salary potential, what interests me about cyber security

These questions are all inclusive, but should serve as a starting point for some things to ask yourself and reflect on. If you have a significant other or a family, it would probably be best to ask their input as well and take it into consideration. Depending on the role, there could be an on-call schedule, certification study, or after hours lab work. These could all impact family life and the traditional work life balance so go ahead, ask their thoughts. If you’re by yourself, then be honest with yourself about motivation and ability to set personal limits since burn out is a real thing. I don’t have any figures but it’s a widespread issue and will kill the excitement of a new change before it even starts.

# Current Information Technology Professionals
If you’re currently in the IT field, then you already have a leg up. A lot of people see the destination and not the journey so they fail (or are maybe unaware) to see and appreciate all of the effort that it takes to achieve those goals. Day to day work or study in your current field may have indirect benefits of easing the transition by allowing an opportunity to develop baseline skills. It is critical as a successful security professional to have a solid foundational knowledge of topics such as how a computer works, the layers of the [OSI model](https://www.networkworld.com/article/3239677/the-osi-model-explained-and-how-to-easily-remember-its-7-layers.html), and how computers and information systems communicate with [networking](https://www.cisco.com/c/en/us/solutions/enterprise-networks/what-is-computer-networking.html).

The goal would be to shift your mindset from “making the technology work” to “how could it be exploited” and “how can I secure this”. It is simply impossible to secure an infrastructure or an asset without having a full insight into the inner workings. Start thinking about events (both good and bad) that you’ve experienced in the past with dealing with the security team. Try to dissect the reasons they were asking the questions they were asking. A lot of this may seem trivial but digging into system hardening and baseline maintenance does more than just make more work for the system administrators.

# Professionals from Other Industries
While I (generally) believe that cyber security isn’t a beginner’s field, I do think that there are roles that can be obtained by beginners looking to get their feet wet. Seeing some posts on [r/ITCareerQuestions](https://www.reddit.com/r/ITCareerQuestions/), there seems to a huge aversion to working in help desk and other entry type roles. I’m not sure if it has to do with the stigma of being overworked and underappreciated or if ego’s get in the way but regardless, it may be necessary to start there. This isn’t all bad, either. The skills that will be gained and honed will play a huge role to future endeavors. One thing that you’ll notice is the lack of soft skills in a good portion of IT workers. By being comfortable and willing to engage with the customers and stakeholders, you get a better appreciation of the business needs, industry, and serves as a networking opportunity within your company.

I’ll go into more detail below, but there are non-technical roles within the field which may also be better suited for beginners. Not everybody has the desire, aptitude, and drive required to be a penetration tester or have the knowledge to tailor and maintain a [SIEM](https://searchsecurity.techtarget.com/definition/security-information-and-event-management-SIEM) or a background in [incident response](https://www.sans.org/reading-room/whitepapers/incident/computer-incident-response-team-641) to be an effective member of the blue team. There are roles within audits and compliance which (to the annoyance of some) don’t require a very technical background but only require a high attention to detail and the ability to speak up if something is wrong.

# Thoughts on Certifications
I personally believe there can be a lot of value derived from certifications. I also want to say that with an asterisk attached. If you use the blueprint that is published by the vendor to tailor your studying, and you put the effort in to learn and apply that knowledge, then I believe certifications are a fantastic way to go and put yourself ahead. This is a great way to generate and add to a self-study plan in order to get to that first step or the next step. 

That being said, if you find yourself being a “paper chaser” where you either utilize brain dumps or try to blow through as many certifications as quickly as possible, then you’re doing a disservice to yourself and ultimately devaluing certifications. To keep this short and sweet, I love certifications and have a few myself but I also believe in getting them ethically and being able to apply the knowledge earned by hard studying and practicing in a home lab with the new technologies learned in order to pound the information. If you don’t want to put in the effort to properly learn the material required for the certification, then I wouldn’t even suggest attempting them. I know this is a fairly polarizing topic within the IT community so please practice your own diligence on the certification’s value.

I'll be doing another post on how to study for certifications in the future.

# Ask for Help
As a transitioning veteran, I got so much value from the insight that was provided from people who have made the move before me and I look for every way to pay that forward. Finding a mentor can provide multiple benefits. By finding the correct mentor in the field or position you want, you will be able to not only network, but receive the insight into how they got to where they are. While every career path is different and the steps that worked for them may not work for you, it will give you a chance to see “behind the curtain” into what they did and what worked for them. 

Another benefit from a mentor would be to get an unfiltered look into the role. Maybe it’s not so pretty from within as it is looking in from the outside. Maybe there are unexpected variables that make it challenging or not a good fit. On the flip side, it could be an extra nudge of motivation and excitement to get to that step if you love what you hear. Biggest take away is get a mentor! Then once you make it to the next stage, help mentor your replacement and pay it forward!
